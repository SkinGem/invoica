### 1. AI Agent Transactions and Autonomous Digital Commerce
- **Jurisdiction: Federal (IRS)**
  - **What Changed/Proposed**: The IRS has not issued specific guidance on AI agent transactions or autonomous digital commerce as of October 2023. However, general tax principles apply: income generated by AI agents (e.g., through automated transactions or services) is taxable as ordinary income or capital gains, depending on the nature of the activity, under Internal Revenue Code (IRC) Section 61.
  - **Effective Date/Status**: Ongoing application of existing tax law; no specific regulation as of 2023.
  - **Impact on Invoica**: Invoica must ensure that AI agent transactions processed on its platform are reported as taxable events for the parties involved (e.g., income to sellers). If AI agents autonomously settle payments in USDC, Invoica may need to track and report these as taxable income or gains under existing IRS rules.
  - **Source**: IRS General Tax Principles (irs.gov).

- **Jurisdiction: State-Level (General)**
  - **What Changed/Proposed**: No state has specific regulations targeting AI agent transactions as of 2023. However, states may treat AI-driven commerce as taxable under existing sales and use tax rules if it involves the sale of goods or taxable services.
  - **Effective Date/Status**: Ongoing.
  - **Impact on Invoica**: Invoica may be required to collect sales tax on transactions facilitated by AI agents if they involve taxable goods or services in states with economic nexus laws (see Topic 5).
  - **Source**: Multistate Tax Commission (mtc.gov).

### 2. Digital Services Tax at Federal and State Levels
- **Jurisdiction: Federal**
  - **What Changed/Proposed**: As of 2023, there is no federal digital services tax (DST) in the U.S. Discussions around a federal DST have been ongoing, influenced by international OECD/G20 agreements on taxing the digital economy (Pillar 1 and Pillar 2), but no legislation has been enacted.
  - **Effective Date/Status**: Not enacted; proposals under discussion.
  - **Impact on Invoica**: If a federal DST is enacted, Invoica could face taxation on revenue from digital services (e.g., transaction processing fees for AI agents), potentially increasing operational costs.
  - **Source**: Congress.gov (ongoing legislative tracking).

- **Jurisdiction: State-Level (CA, NY, TX, FL, WA, IL, MA, NJ, PA, CO)**
  - **Maryland**: Enacted a Digital Advertising Gross Revenues Tax (first state to do so) in 2021, imposing a tax of up to 10% on gross revenues from digital advertising for companies with over $100 million in global annual revenue and $1 million in Maryland digital ad revenue.
    - **Effective Date**: March 2021.
    - **Impact on Invoica**: If Invoica earns revenue from digital advertising, it could be subject to this tax if thresholds are met.
    - **Source**: Maryland Comptroller (comptroller.marylandtaxes.gov).
  - **New York**: Proposed a Digital Services Tax in various legislative sessions (e.g., S.933 in 2023), targeting revenue from digital platforms, though not yet enacted.
    - **Effective Date/Status**: Not enacted as of 2023.
    - **Impact on Invoica**: Potential future tax liability on platform revenue if passed.
    - **Source**: New York State Assembly (nyassembly.gov).
  - **Other States (CA, TX, FL, WA, IL, MA, NJ, PA, CO)**: No specific DSTs enacted as of 2023, though proposals exist in CA and WA for taxing digital services or data usage. CA has considered bills like AB 1780 (2023) for a data extraction tax.
    - **Effective Date/Status**: Not enacted.
    - **Impact on Invoica**: Potential future compliance burden if similar taxes are adopted.
    - **Source**: State tax authority websites (e.g., cdft.ca.gov, dor.wa.gov).
