### 1. AI Agent Transactions and Autonomous Digital Commerce
- **Jurisdiction**: Federal (IRS)
- **What Changed/Proposed**: There is no specific federal tax regulation directly addressing AI agent transactions or autonomous digital commerce as of the latest IRS guidance. However, the IRS treats transactions facilitated by AI or automated systems as taxable events under existing rules for income, sales, or services, depending on the nature of the transaction (e.g., IRC Section 61 for gross income). The IRS has expressed interest in emerging technologies, as noted in the 2023-2024 Priority Guidance Plan, which includes studying digital economy taxation.
- **Effective Date/Status**: Ongoing; no specific rulemaking as of October 2023. The Treasury and IRS are monitoring AI-driven commerce for potential guidance.
- **Impact on Invoica**: Invoica must classify AI agent transactions as taxable events (e.g., service fees or sales) and ensure proper reporting of income under existing IRS rules. Without specific guidance, there is uncertainty about whether AI agents are treated as separate taxable entities or extensions of the platform.
- **Source**: IRS 2023-2024 Priority Guidance Plan (irs.gov); IRC Section 61.

### 2. Digital Services Tax at Federal and State Levels
- **Jurisdiction**: Federal
- **What Changed/Proposed**: No federal digital services tax (DST) exists in the U.S. as of 2023. The U.S. Trade Representative has opposed international DSTs (e.g., in France) and pushed for OECD Pillar 1 agreements on digital economy taxation, which may influence future federal policy.
- **Effective Date/Status**: N/A; OECD negotiations ongoing.
- **Impact on Invoica**: No direct federal DST, but future alignment with OECD rules could impose taxes on digital platforms.
- **Source**: USTR statements (ustr.gov); OECD Pillar 1 updates (oecd.org).

- **Jurisdiction**: State-Level (CA, NY, TX, FL, WA, IL, MA, NJ, PA, CO)
  - **California**: No DST enacted. Proposals for taxing digital services have been discussed in legislative sessions but not passed as of 2023.
  - **New York**: Proposed bills (e.g., S.1975 in 2023) aim to impose taxes on digital advertising and data services, potentially affecting platforms like Invoica if expanded to payment processing.
  - **Texas**: No DST; focuses on sales tax for digital goods under existing rules (Texas Tax Code §151.0035).
  - **Florida**: No DST; sales tax applies to certain digital products (FL Statute §212.05).
  - **Washington**: No DST, but B&O tax applies to digital services (WA Rev. Code §82.04).
  - **Illinois**: No DST; sales tax on digital goods (IL Admin. Code Title 86 §130.1935).
  - **Massachusetts**: No DST; proposed bills for digital advertising tax failed as of 2023.
  - **New Jersey**: No DST; sales tax on digital products (NJ Rev. Stat. §54:32B-2).
  - **Pennsylvania**: No DST; sales tax on digital downloads (PA Rev. Code §7201).
  - **Colorado**: No DST; sales tax on digital goods (CO Rev. Stat. §39-26-102).
- **Effective Date/Status**: Most states have no active DST as of 2023; NY proposals are pending.
- **Impact on Invoica**: Invoica may face state sales taxes on digital services or future DSTs in states like NY, increasing operational costs.
- **Source**: State tax authority websites (e.g., tax.ny.gov, cdtfa.ca.gov); legislative tracking on congress.gov.
