### 1. AI Agent Transactions and Autonomous Digital Commerce
- **Jurisdiction: Federal (IRS)**
  - **What Changed/Proposed**: There is no specific federal tax regulation directly addressing AI agent transactions or autonomous digital commerce as of the latest IRS guidance (October 2023). However, the IRS treats transactions facilitated by AI or automated systems as taxable events under existing rules for digital payments and barter transactions (IRC Section 61). Income generated by AI agents (e.g., invoicing or sales) is reportable by the entity controlling the AI.
  - **Effective Date/Status**: Ongoing interpretation of existing law; no new specific guidance for AI transactions as of 2023.
  - **Impact on Invoica**: Invoica must report income from AI agent transactions as taxable income for the business or individual controlling the agent. Payments settled in USDC may also trigger additional reporting under digital asset rules (see Section 3).
  - **Source**: IRS Publication 525 (Taxable and Nontaxable Income), irs.gov.

- **Jurisdiction: State Level (General)**
  - **What Changed/Proposed**: No state has enacted specific regulations for AI agent transactions as of 2023. States generally follow federal treatment, taxing income from automated transactions as business income.
  - **Effective Date/Status**: N/A.
  - **Impact on Invoica**: Invoica must comply with state income tax rules for business income in jurisdictions where it has nexus (see Section 5 for nexus rules).
  - **Source**: State tax authority websites (e.g., CA Franchise Tax Board, NY Department of Taxation and Finance).

### 2. Digital Services Tax at Federal and State Level
- **Jurisdiction: Federal**
  - **What Changed/Proposed**: No federal digital services tax (DST) exists as of 2023. Discussions in Congress (e.g., via the OECD/G20 Pillar 1 framework) continue regarding taxing digital economy profits, but no legislation has been passed.
  - **Effective Date/Status**: Proposed; no enactment.
  - **Impact on Invoica**: No immediate impact, but future federal DST could impose taxes on revenue from digital invoicing services.
  - **Source**: Congress.gov; OECD Pillar 1 updates.

- **Jurisdiction: State-Level (CA, NY, TX, FL, WA, IL, MA, NJ, PA, CO)**
  - **Maryland (Reference for Other States)**: Maryland’s Digital Advertising Gross Revenues Tax (first in the U.S.) taxes gross revenue from digital advertising at rates of 2.5% to 10% for companies with over $100 million in global revenue. Effective since 2021, it serves as a model for other states.
    - **Effective Date**: March 2021.
    - **Impact on Invoica**: No direct impact unless Invoica engages in digital advertising revenue. However, it signals potential for broader digital service taxes.
    - **Source**: Maryland Comptroller, comptroller.marylandtaxes.gov.
  - **New York**: Proposed legislation (A.7075/S.6746, 2023 session) to impose a digital services tax on large tech companies for data monetization and digital services. Not enacted as of 2023.
    - **Effective Date/Status**: Pending.
    - **Impact on Invoica**: Potential future tax on digital invoicing revenue if expanded to platforms.
    - **Source**: NY State Assembly, nyassembly.gov.
  - **Other States (CA, TX, FL, WA, IL, MA, NJ, PA, CO)**: No enacted DST as of 2023, though CA and WA have proposed bills mirroring Maryland’s model. WA’s HB 1316 (2023) for a digital services tax failed to pass.
    - **Effective Date/Status**: None enacted.
    - **Impact on Invoica**: Monitor for future DST proposals affecting digital platforms.
    - **Source**: State legislative websites; MTC updates, mtc.gov.
